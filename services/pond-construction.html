Pond Construction in Eau Claire, WI

Residential backyard ponds, farm and livestock ponds, stormwater retention ponds, and recreational fishing ponds throughout Chippewa County and the Eau Claire area.

A well-built pond adds real value to a property — for irrigation, livestock, fire suppression, wildlife habitat, or simply the look and feel of having water on the land. Getting the design and excavation right from the start is what determines whether a pond holds water properly for decades or needs constant repair.

Types of Ponds

  • Residential backyard ponds — decorative water features and small recreational ponds
  • Farm and livestock ponds — water sources for cattle, horses, and other livestock
  • Recreational fishing ponds — stocked fishing ponds with proper depth profiles and inlet/outlet structures
  • Stormwater retention ponds — engineered basins that capture runoff from commercial and residential developments
  • Fire suppression ponds — water storage for rural properties and facilities without municipal water access
  • Irrigation ponds — water storage for agricultural irrigation systems

What Pond Construction Involves

  • Site evaluation — assessing soil type, slope, and water source before design begins
  • Excavation — digging the pond basin to the planned depth and shape
  • Clay lining or compaction — many sites need the base compacted or lined with clay to prevent water from seeping out
  • Spillway and overflow design — managing excess water safely during heavy rain so the pond doesn't erode or overflow uncontrolled
  • Grading the surrounding banks — shaping slopes that are stable and safe rather than prone to erosion or collapse

Why Local Soil and Water Conditions Matter

Whether a pond holds water well depends heavily on what's underneath it. Sandy or gravelly soil, common in parts of Chippewa County, drains quickly and often needs a clay liner or bentonite treatment to hold water long-term. Denser clay soil, more common elsewhere in the area, can sometimes hold water on its own with proper compaction. A site's water table also matters — some ponds are spring-fed by a naturally high water table, while others rely entirely on runoff and rainfall to stay full.

What Affects the Cost and Timeline

  • Size and depth of the pond
  • Soil type — whether lining or clay treatment is needed
  • Excavated material — whether it can be used on-site for berms and landscaping or needs to be hauled away
  • Access — how easily equipment can reach the site
  • Permitting — ponds connected to streams, wetlands, or exceeding certain size thresholds often require state or county approval

Because soil and water conditions vary so much by property, the most accurate estimate comes from a contractor evaluating the specific site rather than a general size-based quote.

What to Expect During the Project

Most residential pond excavations take anywhere from a few days to two weeks, depending on size and whether lining work is needed. Construction is generally easiest in drier months, since heavy spring rain and high water tables can complicate digging and grading.

Wisconsin regulates pond construction depending on size, location near wetlands, and whether it connects to a natural waterway — permitting is confirmed before work begins.
